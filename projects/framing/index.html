Framing Effect Simulator

Explore how different presentations of the same information can influence decision-making. The framing effect demonstrates that people often react differently to equivalent information depending on whether it's presented in a positive or negative light.

About This Simulation

In each scenario, both options present statistically equivalent information, just framed differently. Research shows that people tend to be risk-averse when facing positively framed choices and risk-seeking when facing negatively framed choices, even when the outcomes are identical.

Insights & Explanation

What is the Framing Effect?

The framing effect is a cognitive bias where people decide on options based on whether they are presented in a positive or negative way. Identical information can lead to different decisions depending on how it's framed.

Classic Experiment: Asian Disease Problem

In a famous 1981 study by Tversky and Kahneman, participants were presented with a hypothetical outbreak of an Asian disease expected to kill 600 people. When options were framed positively (lives saved), 72% chose the certain option. When framed negatively (lives lost), 78% chose the risky option - even though both scenarios were mathematically identical.

Why Does This Happen?

The framing effect occurs because of loss aversion - people feel losses more intensely than gains of equivalent size. Negatively framed options trigger emotional responses that can override logical analysis of the actual probabilities involved.

Real-World Implications

The framing effect influences decisions in medicine, finance, marketing, and politics. Understanding this bias can help us make more rational decisions and recognize when others might be manipulating our choices through framing.
